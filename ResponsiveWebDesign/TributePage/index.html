A Tribute to

Giuseppe Moscati

(1880-1927),

"Doctor of the Poor"

This website is dedicated to the memory of Giuseppe Moscati, a highly-esteemed physician and scientist from Southern Italy and at the same time a devoted Catholic, who due to his success in treatment and his efforts especially for the most vulnerable and the destitute among his patients has been venerated like a saint since his lifetime. In fact, his veneration did not remain limited to his contemporaries or his homeland: His fame spread quickly far beyond his place of work and has been preserved over several generations, his medical combined with his pastoral commitment have been honored both by secular and by religious institutions. 60 years after his death, Moscati has even been elevated to the ranks of the official saints of the Catholic Church. As a result, right up to the present day a number of believers visit his tomb within the Chapel of the Visitation in the Jesuit church Gesù Nuovo in Naples and offer their prayers (and, occasionally, a votive gift).

For all we know, Moscati must have been an extraordinary person in many respects. Sure enough, he benefited from particularly favourable starting conditions: He grew up well protected in a caring family, he spent a cheerful childhood without experiencing material hardship, he was given the opportunity to develop his talents, received an excellent education and was overall successfully socialised. And yet he did not simply follow the tracks laid out for him, but carved out his very own path in life.

Early on he decided, against his father's expectations, to study medicine rather than law and to pursue a career as a doctor. A keen sense for the suffering in the world, experiences in his family environment and observations in the nearby "Hospital of the Incurables" – his later place of work – are likely to have contributed to this choice, as did his rather practical religiousness (inspired, among others, by St. Francis). Moscati was firmly convinced that he could also save his patients' souls through a holistic, people-centered, empathetic form of treatment – and, if needed, reconcile them with God. And he had all the skills and qualities required for this task.

He not only possessed the necessary technical expertise (which even his enviers and competitors did not dispute), but also a high degree of self-control, of discipline and of willingness to work hard – and to persevere. Moreover, he must have been a good observer and attentive listener, who understood well what was actually going on around him and what people were trying to tell him (or withhold from him). Contemporaries have attested to his exceptional ability to grasp things and to recognise connections, which allowed him to precisely diagnose diseases sometimes even with very incomplete information.

When dealing with his patients, Moscati stood out due to his humanity, his affability, his empathy and his charity, coupled with personal frugality and modesty. As can be seen from a number of his statements, he was prepared to give everything for the well-being and, as he hoped, the salvation of the people entrusted to him. Consequently, he even waived the charges for those indigent and unable to look after themselves and paid for their medication and food out of his own pocket – an engagement that earned him the nickname "doctor of the poor". In accordance with his view of the profession, he did not only provide medical care and issue prescriptions, but also acted as an advocate, a counsellor, a pastor of his patients.

Moscati's Christian stance as a physician, as expressed in this professional ethos, may be seen as another defining aspect of his individual path in life. The man retained his firm faith even in an environment rather critical of religion and notably of traditional Catholicism, working among naturalists, materialists and freemasons alike, whose presence significantly characterised the atmosphere both at the university and at his hospital. For Moscati, who has repeatedly commented on the issue, there was no unbridgeable conflict between religion and science, between the teachings of the Christian faith and the findings of modern scholarship. On the contrary, he saw his own scientific work as an excellent opportunity to serve his fellow men as well as the will of God and, in the final analysis, to rise intellectually to his Creator.

As far as his working environment is concerned, Moscati also endured with equanimity the favouritism and clientelism that he encountered on various occasions mainly in the academic world. And what is more: throughout his life he did never allow himself to be discouraged by setbacks or injustices in the long term, but proved to be amazingly resilient and determined to the end.

Of course, at the end of the day he was only a human, with his individual peculiarities and shortcomings. Moscati was an extremely conservative mind, to whom quite a few of the social and cultural developments of the modern age remained alien (to say the least). He maintained his own strict moral standards and, therefore, took offence at the deviating behaviour of his fellow citizens. Living in Naples not only confronted him day after day with hardship and misery and social tensions, the city also enjoyed a dubious reputation as a „cesspool of sin“ – and, thus, regularly challenged a man of his convictions. Moreover, he was used to placing the highest demands on himself, but, by doing so, also put his supporters and his relatives under pressure to act, who seem to have made great sacrifices for his sake.

In short, despite all his positive character traits (as outlined above), he probably was not always an easy person to get along with: an oftentimes overly serious man, a restless workaholic, an idealist who – deep down – expected a great deal from his contemporaries. And yet, his way of practising the medical profession, his inexhaustible commitment to his fellow human beings and his dedication to his self-chosen mission in life may (alongside his unshakeable trust in God) still inspire people today.

Here is a brief outline of his life, intended as a first orientation to those who have never heard of the man and his achievements:

1880, 25th of July Moscati is born in Benevento, Southern Italy, as the seventh of nine children from a wealthy and distinguished family
1884 due to the father's appointment to the Neapolitan court of appeal, the family moves to Naples
1889-1897 Moscati attends the prestigious secondary school Vittorio Emmanuele II (~ "liceo ginnasio"), with excellent academic results
1892 his elder brother Alberto has a serious riding accident, remains handicapped for the rest of his life (suffering from epileptic seizures, dying in 1904); possibly a first personal incentive for Giuseppe, who takes care of Alberto, to consider a career in medicine
1897 Moscati begins studying medicine at the University of Naples Federico II; two months after his enrollment, his father unexpectedly succumbs to an illness
1903 Moscati passes his final exams with the highest possible score, receives his doctorate from the Faculty of Medicine; soon afterwards he starts working in the famous Ospedale degli Incurabili in the heart of Naples
1906, 4th of April after an eruption of Mount Vesuvius, Moscati earns a high reputation for his commitment to the evacuation and care of patients from the Ospedale of Torre del Greco (= a branch of the Ospedale degli Incurabili), located near the volcano
1908 Moscati starts working (after some time as a volunteer) as a full assistant at the Institute of Physiological Chemistry of the University of Naples Federico II
1911 Moscati gets a job as private lecturer / associate professor ("libera docenza") at the Institute of Physiological Chemistry and is entrusted with the management of its research and laboratory experiments; in the same year, he becomes chief physician at the Ospedale degli Incurabili; his work in the hospital's department of pathology, particularly his treatment of tuberculosis patients and of countless war casualties / invalids during the First World War provide formative experiences; on the occasion of a threatening cholera epidemic in Naples, Moscati is commissioned by the city's public health inspectorate to submit a report on the spread of the disease and possible prophylactic measures – his carefully drafted proposals are at least partially implemented and, in addition to his untiring personal commitment, contribute to saving the lives of many fellow citizens; still in 1911, he travels on behalf of his institute to an international congress for physiology in Vienna; Moscati makes a name for himself through his extensive research work and research management (→ the topics ranging from urogenesis and urea to hepatic/splenic autolysis, glycogen metabolism, tuberculous peritonitis and the lymphatic system), his collaboration on a number of specialist journals, his teaching activities at the university and at the hospital, his medical and administrative work in the latter, and last but not least his unconditional dedication to his patients
1914 after the death of his beloved mother, Moscati converts a room of his flat in the via Cisterna dell'Olio 10, where he lives with his sister Anna, into an examination room and starts receiving patients even outside his regular working hours
1915 for the First World War period (1915-1918), Moscati is appointed director of his hospital's Military Department (after his application for a voluntary enrolment for military service was rejected)
1916/1920 Moscati gets a series of teaching assignments in physiology and physiological chemistry at the University of Naples
1919 Moscati becomes a full lecturer in clinical chemistry at the University of Naples
1922 Moscati becomes a private lecturer / associate professor ("libera docenza") in clinical medicine at the University of Naples; in the same year he is one of the first doctors to successfully use insulin therapy to treat diabetes
1923 Moscati attends another international congress for physiology, this time in Edinburgh – an occasion for an extended journey via Rome, Turin, Paris, London and Lourdes, among other places
1927, 12th of April Moscati dies at the age of 46 in Naples: after attending Holy Mass and his daily rounds with the patients, he retires and falls asleep for good
1930, 16th of November his remains are transferred from the cemetery of Poggioreale to the Jesuit church Gesù Nuovo, with great participation of the population
1975, 16th of November Moscati is beatified by pope Paul VI.
1987, 25th of October Moscati is canonised by pope John Paul II.
↪ In his homily for the occasion, the pope noted:
"The man whom we will invoke from today as a saint of the universal church, presents himself to us as a concrete realisation of the ideal of the Christian layman. […] Moscati provides an example not only to be admired, but to be imitated, especially by health care workers. […] He sets himself as an example even for those who do not share his faith. However, it was precisely this faith that gave his commitment new dimensions and qualities, those typical of the authentically Christian layman. Thanks to them, the professional aspects, in his life, harmoniously complemented each other, supported each other, to be lived as a response to a vocation, and thus as a collaboration to God's creative and redemptive plan. [...]
Mindful of the Lord's words, 'I was sick and you visited me' (Mt 25:36), Moscati saw Christ himself in the sick person, who, in his weakness, his misery, his frailty and insecurity, turned to him calling for help; he saw the one facing him as a person, a being in whom there was a body in need of care, but also a being in whom dwelt a spirit likewise in need of help and comfort."
2018 after the collapse of a wall in the Neapolitan apartment where Moscati lived for many years and where he also died, the remaining furnishings are removed to the church Gesù Nuovo (the site of his tomb), where they can be viewed today

Selected Quotes of Moscati

What can humans actually do? What can they do against the eternal laws of life? Hence the need for refuge in God. And yet we doctors have to try to alleviate the suffering.

Giuseppe Moscati
